How to Increase Your Typing Speed: A Complete Guide

Most people plateau at 40–55 WPM and never break through. Here's exactly why — and how to fix it.

By SpeedType Arena June 29, 2025 10 min read

The average office worker types at around 40 words per minute. Professional typists clock 65–75 WPM. The top competitive typists reach 150 WPM and beyond. The difference between these groups is not innate talent — it's technique, deliberate practice, and an understanding of what actually makes typing fast.

Why Most People Plateau

The typing speed plateau is one of the most frustrating experiences in skill development. You practice regularly, yet your WPM barely moves. The reason is almost always the same: you're reinforcing your existing habits instead of changing them.

Typing is a motor skill, which means it is governed by muscle memory — patterns your fingers have learned to execute automatically. When you type the same way you've always typed, you build the same muscle memory you've always had. Speed requires new patterns, and new patterns require conscious, uncomfortable change before they become automatic.

The good news: it doesn't take long. Most people who apply the techniques below see measurable improvement within two to three weeks of focused practice.

Step 1: Fix Your Posture and Ergonomics

Before touching technique, address your physical setup. Poor ergonomics create tension that limits your hand speed and causes fatigue.

  • Sit up straight with your back supported. Slouching compresses your chest and restricts arm movement.
  • Position your keyboard so your elbows are at roughly 90 degrees and your forearms are parallel to the floor or angled slightly downward.
  • Keep your wrists off the desk while typing. Wrist rests are for resting between bursts, not during active typing. Resting wrists on the desk while typing creates friction and limits finger reach.
  • Place the monitor at eye level — looking down at a screen compresses your neck and throws your posture out of alignment.
  • Relax your shoulders and hands. Tension is the enemy of speed. If your hands feel tight, shake them out and start again.

Step 2: Master the Home Row

The home row is the foundation of fast typing. The fingers of your left hand rest on A, S, D, F — and the right hand on J, K, L, and the semicolon. Your thumbs rest on the spacebar.

Every key on the keyboard is reached by moving from these anchor positions and immediately returning. This is what makes touch typing fast: your fingers always know where they are. Without home row anchoring, each keystroke requires a small moment of visual confirmation — and those moments add up to a significant WPM penalty.

If you don't currently type with home row anchoring, this is the single change with the highest ROI. Spend one week practicing only with proper finger placement, even if it feels slow at first. The temporary speed loss is an investment in permanent speed gains.

Step 3: Stop Looking at the Keyboard

Looking at the keyboard is a habit, not a necessity. Once you develop muscle memory for key locations, your fingers know where to go without visual confirmation — but the habit of looking can persist long after the need for it has passed.

The fastest way to break this habit: cover your hands while typing. Use a cloth, a piece of cardboard, or simply train yourself to look only at the screen. It will feel deeply uncomfortable for the first few sessions. That discomfort is precisely the signal that you're building new neural pathways.

Commit to one week of screen-only focus, even if it means temporarily typing more slowly and making more errors. The accuracy improvement comes quickly, and speed follows within days.

Step 4: Use All Ten Fingers

Many self-taught typists rely on four to six fingers rather than ten. Each unused finger represents a portion of your keyboard that requires reaching with a longer finger, which is slower and less accurate.

The correct finger assignment for a standard QWERTY keyboard:

  • Left pinky: Q, A, Z, and the left modifier keys
  • Left ring finger: W, S, X
  • Left middle finger: E, D, C
  • Left index finger: R, F, V, T, G, B
  • Right index finger: Y, H, N, U, J, M
  • Right middle finger: I, K, comma
  • Right ring finger: O, L, period
  • Right pinky: P, semicolon, slash, and right modifier keys
  • Both thumbs: Spacebar

If any of these feel unnatural, you've identified a weakness to train. Focus single-key-row exercises on those specific fingers.

Step 5: Practice Deliberately, Not Casually

There is an enormous difference between casual practice and deliberate practice. Casual practice — just typing a lot — builds modest improvement. Deliberate practice — systematically targeting your weaknesses — builds rapid improvement.

Here's what deliberate typing practice looks like:

  • Identify your slowest key combinations. After each test session, notice which words or character pairs tripped you up. Write them down.
  • Isolate those combinations. Type them slowly and correctly 20–30 times before increasing speed. Accuracy before speed — always.
  • Use short, focused sessions. 10–15 minutes of genuinely focused practice beats an hour of distracted typing. Motor skill learning degrades with fatigue.
  • Practice slightly above your comfort zone. If you can comfortably type at 50 WPM, practice at text difficulty that challenges you at 55–60 WPM. The slightly elevated difficulty zone is where growth happens.
  • Vary your practice material. Typing the same sentences repeatedly builds narrow muscle memory. Varying text ensures broad vocabulary coverage.

Step 6: Use a Consistent Rhythm

Fast typists don't type in bursts — they type in flows. A consistent, rhythmic keystroke pace is more efficient than alternating between sprints and pauses. Think of it like running: the most efficient long-distance runners maintain an even pace rather than sprinting and recovering.

A useful exercise is to practice typing to a metronome at a tempo that represents one keystroke per beat, then gradually increasing the BPM. This builds rhythmic consistency that manifests as higher WPM during actual timed tests.

Step 7: Test and Track Your Progress

Consistent measurement is essential for improvement. Without baseline data, you can't tell whether your practice is working. Take a standardized 60-second typing test at the beginning of each week and record your results.

Track both WPM and accuracy. Many typists see WPM improve while accuracy degrades — this is a sign of speed without consolidation, and it typically regresses. Aim to keep accuracy above 95% during practice sessions; prioritize accuracy over speed until the accuracy is automatic.

Realistic Progress Benchmarks

With dedicated daily practice of 15–20 minutes, here's what you can realistically expect:

  • Week 1–2: Speed may temporarily decrease as you correct bad habits. This is normal and expected.
  • Week 3–4: Speed returns to baseline with noticeably better accuracy.
  • Month 2: Speed typically improves 10–20 WPM above your previous ceiling.
  • Month 3+: Continued improvement, with diminishing returns as you approach 80+ WPM. Each additional 10 WPM requires more practice than the last.

Conclusion

Increasing your typing speed is a learnable skill available to anyone willing to practice correctly. The most important insight is this: typing faster requires changing how you type, not just doing more of the same. Fix your posture, anchor to home row, use all ten fingers, stop looking at the keyboard, and practice deliberately. The WPM gains will follow.
